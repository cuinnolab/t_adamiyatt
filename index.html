Tahmouress Adamiyatt

01 Biography
Early Life & Education

Tahmouress Adamiyatt was born in Tehran, Iran into a family distinguished by intellectual and public service. He received his undergraduate and law degrees from Tehran University, entering a profession that reflected the reformist currents coursing through Iranian society in the interwar years. Before joining the foreign service, he practiced as a lawyer and served in the Iranian army during World War II, gaining an early experience of geopolitics at one of history's most volatile moments.

Diplomatic Career

Ambassador Adamiyatt entered the Iranian foreign service in 1942, embarking on a career that would span four decades and touch the Soviet Union, Eastern Europe, and the United Kingdom. His longest and most consequential posting was to Moscow, where he witnessed the final years of Stalinist rule, the Khrushchev thaw, and the complex triangulation of Iranian-Soviet-American Cold War politics. He is remembered as a careful, methodical diplomat who signed a nuclear nonproliferation agreement between Iran and the Soviet Union during his ambassadorial tenure.

Family & Legacy

He belonged to a remarkable family of statesmen. His brother Fereydun Adamiyatt — one of Iran's foremost historians — served as ambassador to India, the Netherlands, and the United Nations, and became celebrated for his scholarship on the Iranian Constitutional Revolution. Two other brothers, Manoutchehr and Houshang, also maintained prominent public lives.

After the 1979 Iranian Revolution, Tahmouress relocated to the Washington D.C. area, where he devoted his later years to writing memoirs and historical studies on the Iranian Constitution. He was survived by his late wife Razieh and two sons, Ashk and Saman. He was resident in Chevy Chase, Maryland at the time of his passing.

The Iranian Oral History Project was launched at Harvard's Center for Middle Eastern Studies in the autumn of 1981, founded by historian Habib Ladjevardi to preserve the personal accounts of Iranians who had shaped or witnessed the country's turbulent twentieth century — from the Constitutional Revolution through the fall of the Pahlavi monarchy in 1979.

Originally recorded on cassette tapes — now held at the Harvard Depository — with paper transcripts at Houghton Library, the interviews were digitized and made publicly available through Harvard's CURIOSity digital platform. The collection comprises nearly 900 items, the vast majority conducted in Persian, and constitutes one of the most substantial primary source archives on modern Iranian political history anywhere in the world. Copies are also held at the Bodleian Library, Oxford.

Adamiyatt's 15 tapes were recorded over four days in February 1984 in Bethesda, Maryland, by interviewer Zia Sedghi. They stand among the collection's most significant diplomatic testimonies, offering a firsthand view of Iran-Soviet relations across two decades of Cold War history.
